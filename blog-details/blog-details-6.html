Valentine's Day Flowers: Beyond Red Roses

Red roses are the most recognised romantic symbol on earth. But there are people in your life whose love doesn't fit neatly into a dozen red stems — and there are feelings that roses, for all their eloquence, cannot fully express. This Valentine's Day, let us help you find exactly the right flower for exactly the right love.

First — The Case For Red Roses

We should be honest: red roses are iconic for a reason. They carry centuries of unambiguous meaning, they're universally understood, they photograph magnificently, and a beautifully arranged red rose bouquet has a visual impact that very few other arrangements can match. If you're in a passionate, deep romantic relationship and you want to communicate that clearly and powerfully — there is nothing wrong with red roses. They remain our most-ordered Valentine's arrangement every year.

But love is more varied than one flower. And the people you love — the friend who held you through a hard year, the partner of twenty years, the new love you're still figuring out — deserve flowers that speak specifically to that love.

For New Love: Tulips

Tulips are the perfect flower for a relationship that is still finding its shape. They carry meaning — desire, perfect love, new beginnings — without the weight and intensity of roses. A bunch of soft pink or coral tulips on a first or second Valentine's Day says: I am genuinely enchanted by you, and I'm excited to see where this goes. It's romantic without being overwhelming.

The practical bonus: tulips are striking and beautiful even in simple supermarket wrapping. A perfectly chosen bunch of tulips, hand-carried, often reads as more romantic than an expensive arranged bouquet that was clearly ordered online.

Colour Guide for New Love

Soft pink tulips — "I care for you." Coral tulips — "You make me happy." White tulips — "I'm thinking of a future with you." Avoid yellow tulips in early romance — they traditionally signal sunshine and friendship more than desire.

For Long, Deep Love: Peonies

Peonies are the flower of romance, prosperity, and — crucially for long-term relationships — good fortune in marriage. Their lush, layered petals and extraordinary fragrance make them feel genuinely luxurious, and they carry a depth and complexity that mirrors the kind of love built over years.

For a partner of five, ten, or twenty years, peonies say something that roses simply don't: This is abundant. This is layered. This has grown into something extraordinary. A full peony bouquet in blush pink, coral, and deep rose, wrapped simply in brown paper and raffia, is one of the most beautiful things we make. And its fragrance will fill a room for days.

Peonies are the flower of a love that has been tested and has flourished. They feel like a long marriage — deeply layered, abundantly beautiful, and more meaningful the longer you look.

— Meera Nair, Floral Care Specialist, Rose & Stem

For Friendship: Sunflowers and Yellow Freesias

Valentine's Day is, at its best, a celebration of love in all its forms — and the friends who have been there through everything deserve flowers as much as romantic partners do. Sunflowers are the most emotionally generous flower: they turn towards light, they're abundant and open, they communicate joy and loyalty and warmth without any romantic ambiguity.

Yellow freesias add a delicate fragrance and a beautiful texture alongside sunflowers. Together they make an arrangement that says: You have been a consistent source of light in my life and I am profoundly grateful for you. Which, frankly, is one of the most important things one person can say to another.

For Yourself: Whatever You Love Most

This is genuinely important: Valentine's Day is also a perfectly valid occasion to buy yourself flowers. There is nothing more self-respecting and affirming than standing in a florist (or placing an order online) and choosing something beautiful — not for anyone else, not as a gesture, but simply because you find them beautiful and you deserve to live with beautiful things.

What flower do you love most, when no one else's opinion is involved? Order that one. Put it somewhere you'll see it every morning. The ritual of caring for living flowers — changing the water, recutting the stems, watching them open — is a form of self-care that genuinely affects mood and mental state. Science confirms it; experience confirms it.

The Research on Flowers and Wellbeing

Studies by Rutgers University and the University of North Florida have found that the presence of fresh flowers in a home measurably reduces anxiety, increases positive emotion, and improves connection with others. The effect is strongest when flowers are received unexpectedly — including from yourself.

For an Apology: White Tulips and Purple Hyacinth

Valentine's Day falls with painful irony for some couples in the middle of a difficult period. If you need to say sorry this February — genuinely, specifically, and completely — white tulips carry the meaning of forgiveness and new beginnings. Purple hyacinth is the traditional flower of asking for forgiveness. Together they make an arrangement that is honest and soft rather than deflecting with extravagant romance.

As always: the note matters more than the flowers. The flowers open the conversation. What you write determines whether it's accepted.

For a Mother or Grandmother: Lilies and White Orchids

Valentine's Day is an excellent occasion to acknowledge the maternal love in your life — and this love deserves its own floral language. White lilies carry elegance and purity. White phalaenopsis orchids last for weeks and feel genuinely luxurious and considered. Together they make an arrangement that says: I recognise you, I honour you, and I am grateful for the love you have given consistently and quietly all these years.

Whatever you choose this Valentine's Day — roses or tulips, peonies or sunflowers, something for a partner or something for yourself — choose it with full attention. Because the real gift isn't the flower. It's the moment of recognition it represents: that you saw someone, that you thought of them, and that you put something beautiful and living in their hands to say so.
